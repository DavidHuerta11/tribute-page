The life of the Buddha

"No one saves us but ourselves. No one can and no one may. We ourselves must walk the path."

He Who Achieves His Aim

Siddhartha lived sometime between the 6th to 4th century B.C. There is still much debate as to the exact dates, but what is known as fact is that this important person of history actually existed.

It is agreed upon that Siddhartha was born in Lumbini in present day Nepal. Born the son of the ruler of the Shakya clan he never lacked for anything. His life up to adulthood was of worldy seclusion thanks to his father who wished to protect him from the knowledge of religion, human hardship and the outside world.

He had little to no experience of the world until one day he ventured outside the palace walls and was quickly confronted with the realities of human frailty: disease, old age, and death. This impact of these experiences drove him to search for a way to relieve universal suffrage.

Siddhartha began to live an ascetic life, studying and meditating using the words of various religious teachers as his guide. However, no matter how much effort, pain, and fasting he endured his questions went unanswered. Whatever he tried, Siddhartha could not reach the level of insight he sought. Until one day he realized, thanks to a girl who offered him rice, that corporeal austerity was not the means to achieve inner liberation, and that living under harsh physical constraints was not helping him achieve spiritual release.

That night, Siddhartha sat alone under the Bodhi tree, vowing to not get up until the truths he sought came to him, and he meditated until the sun came up the next day. He remained there for several days, purifying his mind, seeing his entire life, and previous lives, in his thoughts.

Soon a picture began to form in his mind of all that occurred in the universe, and Siddhartha finally saw the answer to the questions of suffering that he had been seeking for so many years. In that moment of pure enlightenment, Siddhartha Gautama became the Buddha.

The Awakened One

Siddhartha encouraged the 5 ascetics he had practiced with to follow a path of balance instead of one characterized by either aesthetic extremism or sensuous indulgence. He called this path the Middle Way. He preached his first sermon (henceforth known as Setting in Motion the Wheel of the Dharma), in which he explained the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path, which became the pillars of Buddhism.

The ascetics became his first disciples and formed the foundation of the Sangha, or community of monks. Women were admitted to the Sangha, and all barriers of class, race, sex and previous background were ignored, with only the desire to reach enlightenment through the banishment of suffering and spiritual emptiness considered.

For the remainder of his years, Buddha traveled, preaching the Dharma (the name given to his teachings) in an effort to lead others along the path of enlightenment.
